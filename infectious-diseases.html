Chikungunya

Mosquito-borne viral illness that causes sudden fever, severe joint pain, rash, headache, and fatigue, often spread by infected Aedes mosquitoes

Summary

  • Type: viral, arbovirus (vector-borne illness), acute febrile illness
  • Symptoms: fever, severe joint pain, muscle pain, headache, nausea, fatigue, rash, 20% asymptomatic
  • Symptoms (rare): neurological, haemorrhagic, occular symptoms
  • Incubation Period: 4-8 days (sometimes 2-12 days)
  • Transmission: vector, intrapartum transmission, laboratory transmission, blood transfusion, other bloodborne exposures
  • Vector: Aedes mosquitoes (aegypti & albopictus)
  • High-Risk Populations: newborns, elderly people, people with chronic medical conditions
  • High-Risk Regions: tropical and sub-tropical countries, urban settings and crowded environments
  • High-Risk Time: biting during the day, picking around sunrise and sunset
  • Emerging Because: urbanization
  • Duration: acute: 3-10 days; 40% of patients have ongoing joint pain or arthritis can last for months or years.
  • Misdiagnozed by: dengue, malaria, zika, leptospirosis, meningitis, rheumatic fever
  • Diagnosis: molecular tests (RT-PCR), serological tests (ELISA)

Treatment

  • Currently no specific antiviral drug
  • Antipyretics, optimal analgesics and fluids to relieve symptoms
  • Applying cold compresses
  • Paracetamol for symptom relief
  • Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) used when diagnosis of dengue is excluded
  • Long-term anti-inflammatory therapy for prolonged convalescence
  • All suspected and confirmed cases should use insecticide-treated bed netsduring the febrile period
  • Emotional and psychosocial support for patients and their families
  • Rehabilitation (occupational and social), counseling support, pain management, and physiotherapy for people with chronic symptoms

Prevention

  • Vaccination
  • Strong surveillance, including adequate patient testing and mosquito vector surveillance (both larval and adult)
  • Vector control strategies, such as source reduction and larviciding (in airports, according to IHR 2005)
  • Community engagement, such as clean-up campaigns
  • Community education: reducing contact with mosquitoes, eliminating mosquito breeding sites
  • Personal protective measures: wearing clothes that minimize skin exposure, use of repellents, windows screens, and air conditioning
  • Insecticide-treated bed nets
  • Solid floors and roofs
  • Research on reasons for large outbreaks interspersed by periods of prolonged absence, virus survival in nature, and factors triggering outbreaks

Cholera

Bacterial infection of the intestines caused by Vibrio cholerae, usually spread through contaminated water or food, leading to severe watery diarrhea and dehydration

Summary

  • Type: bacterial, water-borne illness, acute diarrheal infection, easily treatable
  • Pathogen: Vibrio cholerae
  • Caused by: consuming contaminated food or water, limited access to safe water, basic sanitation facilities, poor hygiene practices
  • Environmental Factors:climate events such as cyclones, floods, and drought
  • Importance:indicates inequity and lack of social and economic development; can be fatal in hours if untreated
  • Symptoms: severe acute watery diarrhea, dehydration; or asymptomatic who can transfer the bacteria through faeces for 1-10 days
  • Incubation Period: 12 hours to 5 days
  • High-Risk Region: South Asia
  • Diagnisos: laboratory testing by culture, seroagglutination or polymerase chain reaction (PCR)

Treatment

  • Prompt oral rehydration solution (ORS)
  • Intravenous fluids, ORS and antibiotics for severe disease
  • Community access to ORS during cholera outbreaks
  • chemoprophylaxis is not recommended, it may contribute to antimicrobial resistance

Prevention

  • Access to safe drinking water
  • Improving WASH (water, sanitation, and hygiene)
  • Strong epidemiological and laboratory surveillance
  • Increasing risk communication and community engagement
  • Safe preparation and storage of food and water
  • Handwashing with soap and water
  • Safe disposal of faeces
  • Economic development
  • Improving access to quality treatment
  • Implementing oral cholera vaccine (OCV) campaigns
  • OCV: Dukoral, Euvichol-Plus, and Euvichol-S (it's safe during pregnancy)
  • One dose of uvichol-Plus or Euvichol-S provides good short-term protection (over one year old), and two doses of both to fully protect an adult
  • Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs)
  • Water quality monitoring
  • Improving WASH in healthcare facilities

Diphtheria

Serious bacterial infection caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae that mainly affects the throat and nose, producing a toxin that can damage the heart, nerves, and other organs

Summary

  • Type: bacterial
  • Pathogen: Corynebacterium diphtheriae
  • Target: respiratory system tissues
  • Symptoms: sore throat, fever, swollen neck glands, weakness
  • Symptoms (Severe): after 2-3 days, nose, tonsils and throat become gray, hard breathing and swallowing, damage to the heart & kidney & nerves
  • Transmission: respiratory droplets from coughing or sneezing, touching infected open sores or ulcers
  • High-Risk Populations: household contacts, those exposed to secretions from the patient, those with frequent and close contact with the patient

Treatment

  • Antibiotics
  • Diphtheria antitoxin

Prevention

  • Vaccination (included in essential immunization programs, WHO: 3 doze primary vaccination followed by 3 boosted doses, it has side effects)

Ebola Virus Disease (EVD)

Severe and often fatal illness in humans caused by the Ebola virus, spreading through direct contact with infected bodily fluids and leading to symptoms such as fever, bleeding, organ failure, and shock

Summary

  • Type: viral (EVD), has sudden symptoms
  • Pathogen: orthoebolavirus
  • Natural Hosts: fruit bats of the Pteropodidae family
  • Transmission: wild animals, direct contact with blood, secretions, organs, other body fluids, contaminated surfaces and materials
  • Case Fatality Rate: 50% (25-90%)
  • Symptoms (Sudden): fever, fatigue, malaise, muscle pain, headache, sore throat
  • Symptoms (Later): vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, rash, impaired kidney and liver functions
  • Symptoms in Rare Cases: internal and external bleeding (e.g. oozing from the gums, blood in the stools)
  • Incubation Period: 2-21 days (cannot spread the disease before symptoms develop)
  • High-Risk Regions: remote villages in Central Africa, near tropical rainforests (first outbreak), west africa (largest outbreak)
  • Misdiagnozed by: malaria, typhoid fever, meningitis

Treatment

  • Early Intensive Supportive Care: rehydration with oral or intravenous fluids, treatment of specific symptoms
  • monoclonal antibodies mAb114 (ansuvimabTM) or REGN-EB3 (InmazebTM) for EVD

Prevention

  • Ervebo, Zabdeno, and Mvabea vaccines for Ebola Virus Disease
  • The Ervebo vaccine is recommended as part of outbreak response
  • Infection prevention and control in health facilities
  • Safe and dignified burials
  • Community engagement

Crimean-Congo Haemorrhagic Fever (CCHF)

Severe tick-borne viral disease that causes sudden fever, muscle pain, and potentially life-threatening internal and external bleeding in humans

Summary

  • Type: viral
  • Transmission: ticks, contact with viraemic animal tissues
  • Case-Fatality Ratio: 30% (10-40%), death occurs in the second week
  • Incubation Period: 1-3 days, maximum of 9 days
  • Incubation Period if Contacted with Infected Blood or Tissues: 5-6 days, maximum of 13 days
  • Symptoms (Sudden): fever, muscle ache, dizziness, neck pain, backache, headache, sore eyes, photophobia
  • Symptoms (Probable): nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, sore throat, followed by sharp mood swings and confusion
  • Symptoms (After 2-4 Days): agitation may be replaced by sleepiness, depression, lassitude, probable upper right abdominal pain, detectable hepatomegaly (liver enlargement)
  • Symptoms (Severe):rapid kidney deterioration, sudden liver failure, pulmonary failure after fifth day of illness
  • High-Risk Regions: Endemic in all of Africa, the Balkans, the Middle East, and Asia

Treatment

  • Early Intensive Supportive Care: fluid management, treatment of specific symptoms

Prevention

  • Tick control with acaricides
  • Public education

Measles

Highly contagious viral disease that causes fever, cough, runny nose, inflamed eyes, and a distinctive red rash, and it can lead to serious complications especially in unvaccinated people

Summary

  • Type: viral, highly contagious
  • Symptoms: high fever, runny nose, bloodshot eyes, tiny white spots on the inside of the mouth
  • Symptoms (After Several Days) rash develops, starting on the face and upper neck and gradually spreading downwards
  • Symptoms (Severe): blindness, encephalitis, severe diarrhea, dehydration, severe respiratory infections such as pneumonia
  • Transmission: droplets from the nose, mouth, or throat
  • Incubation Period: 10-14 days
  • High-Risk Populations: children, especially: poorly nourished, with insufficient vitamin A, weakened immune system by HIV/AIDS
  • High-Risk Regions: low-income countries, especially in Africa and Asia

Treatment

  • Relieve symptoms: fever-reducing medications such as paracetamol or ibuprofen
  • Vitamin A for children

Prevention

  • Routine measles vaccination for children (2 doses)
  • Mass immunization campaigns in countries with low routine coverage
